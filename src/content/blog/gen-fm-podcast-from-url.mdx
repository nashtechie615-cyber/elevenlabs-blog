Paste a link. Wait about a minute. Listen to two AI hosts debate, summarize, and riff on whatever you just threw at them. That is the basic pitch for GenFM, the podcast-generation feature built into [ElevenReader](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4). It sounds like a party trick, but it turns out to be genuinely useful once you start folding it into a real workflow.

## [What GenFM Actually Does](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

GenFM takes a URL — a news article, a research paper, a long-form essay, a blog post — and converts it into a short conversational podcast. Not a robotic text-to-speech reading. An actual back-and-forth between two distinct AI voices, where one host explains a point and the other pushes back, asks a follow-up, or adds context.

The two-host format matters more than it might seem. A single narrator reading text keeps you passive. A conversation forces the content into a structure where ideas get challenged and clarified. That compression tends to stick better than raw reading.

You do not need to configure anything. Paste the URL, hit generate, and ElevenReader handles the script writing and voice synthesis together. The audio is listenable — not clipped or robotic — because it runs on the same voice engine that powers the rest of the ElevenLabs platform.

## [Who Gets the Most Out of This](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Three groups tend to find the most immediate value here.

**Researchers** deal with a constant backlog of papers and articles. Reading everything closely is not realistic. GenFM lets you run a paper through as audio first, get the shape of the argument, and then decide whether it deserves a deeper read. That triage saves real time.

**Students** can take assigned readings and turn them into something that fits into a commute or a gym session. The conversational format also helps when the source material is dense — having two hosts work through a concept together often clarifies things faster than rereading a paragraph.

**Commuters** are the obvious third group. If you have forty-five minutes in a car or on a train, you can burn through three or four articles you would otherwise never get to. The format holds attention better than a plain reading because it moves like actual conversation.

## [A Few Practical Notes](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Not every URL works perfectly. Paywalled content will produce limited results, and very short pages may not give the hosts enough material to work with. Longer articles and papers with clear structure tend to generate the strongest episodes.

The generated audio lives inside ElevenReader, so you can queue up several pieces and work through them across a day. There is no separate app to manage. If you already use ElevenReader for standard text-to-speech, GenFM is just another mode inside the same interface.

It is also worth noting that GenFM does not replace reading. It is a front door. Use it to figure out what deserves more attention, not as a substitute for actually engaging with material that matters.
