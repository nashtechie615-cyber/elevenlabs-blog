Three tools. One ugly recording — shot outdoors, wind hitting the mic, an AC unit humming in the background, and traffic bleeding in from half a block away. The goal was simple: which tool actually cleans it up without wrecking the voice underneath?

## [What the Test Actually Involved](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

The source file was a 90-second interview clip recorded on a smartphone. No lapel mic, no windscreen, no controlled environment. Exactly the kind of audio that shows up in real-world production all the time.

Each tool got the same unprocessed WAV. Output was judged on three things: how much noise was removed, whether the voice held its natural character, and how much effort the whole process took.

**ElevenLabs Voice Isolator** handled the file in under a minute through a clean drag-and-drop interface. The wind and AC hum came down sharply. More importantly, the voice stayed warm — no metallic edge, no over-smoothing. It didn't try to do anything clever with EQ or compression. It just separated the voice from everything else and handed it back. For a tool focused on a single job, that's exactly what you want.

**Descript Studio Sound** sits inside a full editing environment, which is either a feature or friction depending on your workflow. The noise reduction was solid, but the processing added a slight brightness to the upper mids that wasn't in the original. Not a dealbreaker, but noticeable on headphones. Getting to the feature required importing the file into a project, which adds steps if you just want a clean audio file and nothing else.

**Adobe Enhance Speech** (part of Adobe Podcast) did a respectable job on the steady-state noise. The AC hum practically disappeared. Where it struggled was the intermittent traffic bursts — they got softened but not removed, and the voice took on a processed quality that some people find clinical. The web interface is fast and free to access, which matters for occasional users.

## [Where Each Tool Earns Its Spot](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Pick **ElevenLabs Voice Isolator** when output quality is the priority and you're not interested in a longer editing session. It's built for this exact task. [Check it out here](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) if you produce podcasts, interviews, or voiceover work with inconsistent recording conditions.

Pick **Descript** when you're already editing inside their platform and want noise cleanup baked into a larger workflow. The extra steps are less painful if you're spending an hour in the editor anyway.

Pick **Adobe Enhance** when cost is the deciding factor and the recording problems are mostly consistent background noise rather than complex layered interference. It's a strong free option with a low barrier to entry.

## [The Bottom Line](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

No single tool wins every scenario, but ElevenLabs Voice Isolator came out ahead on the thing that matters most: the voice sounded like itself after processing. That's harder to achieve than it looks, and most tools sacrifice some naturalness to hit the noise floor target.
