The old workflow was painful in a familiar way: generate a paragraph, listen back, hate one sentence, regenerate the whole block, lose three good takes in the process. Studio 2.x worked, but it worked the way a word processor works — linear, blunt, and indifferent to the fact that audio has *time*. Studio 3.0 is a different animal. It borrows enough from professional audio production that editors who've spent time in DAWs will feel the logic immediately.

## [Chapter Structure Changes How You Think About Long-Form](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

The biggest structural shift in 3.0 is the chapter system. Long-form projects — audiobooks, multi-segment podcasts, extended narration — can now be broken into discrete chapters that each carry their own settings. Voice assignments, pacing, and pronunciation dictionaries can differ between chapters without affecting the rest of the project.

In practice, this means you can have a narrator voice for body text and a separate character voice for dialogue, each contained in their own chapter, without any manual splicing after the fact. The project stays in one place. You stop exporting fragments and stitching them together in a separate tool.

For audiobook producers especially, this alone justifies upgrading. Chapter-level organization maps directly to how the source material is already structured.

## [Timeline Scrubbing and the Regenerate-Just-This-Sentence Button](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

The timeline view is where 3.0 earns its DAW comparison most directly. You can scrub through audio in the editor the way you'd scrub through a track in Logic or Reaper — click anywhere on the waveform, hear what's there, move on. No more playing from the top to find the one awkward pause in paragraph six.

The sentence-level regeneration feature is the other major quality-of-life improvement. Select a single sentence, hit regenerate, and only that segment gets replaced. The audio around it stays intact. [This is the feature](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) that makes iterative editing practical instead of theoretical.

Combined with waveform display directly in the edit view, you can now make decisions visually. A trailing breath, an unusually short pause, an overlong gap — they're visible before you even press play.

## [Pronunciation Overrides Are Now First-Class](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Pronunciation dictionaries existed before 3.0, but they were awkward to apply and easy to lose between sessions. In 3.0, pronunciation overrides are scoped to the project and persist across all regenerations. You define how a proper noun, technical term, or brand name should sound once, and it holds.

You can use IPA notation or a phonetic respelling, depending on what you're comfortable with. The override applies everywhere that word appears in the project — you don't have to tag each instance manually.

For anyone producing content with specialized vocabulary (medical narration, legal documents, technical training material), [this feature](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) removes a persistent source of frustration that made professional-grade output harder to achieve at scale.
