Most voice clones sound like a phone call from 2003. Flat, buzzy, weirdly paced. The model isn't always to blame — the recording is. Garbage in, garbage out applies to audio just as much as it does to data. If you want a clone that actually sounds like you, the work happens before you ever upload a file.

## [Gear That's Good Enough (and Gear That Isn't)](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

You don't need a professional studio microphone. You do need something better than your laptop's built-in mic. A USB condenser in the $80–$120 range — the kind podcasters use — picks up enough detail without introducing too much noise. Dynamic microphones work too, especially if your room isn't treated, because they reject more ambient sound by design.

What kills a recording: fan noise, HVAC hum, and room reverb. Reverb is the sneaky one. A voice clone trained on a reverberant recording will reproduce that reverb baked into every sentence. It won't sound like a room — it'll sound like a broken effect.

Budget room treatment doesn't require foam panels. Record inside a closet full of clothes. Hang a heavy blanket behind you. Sit under a duvet if you have to. Soft materials absorb reflections. Hard surfaces bounce them back into your microphone.

## [What to Record and How Long](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

[ElevenLabs](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) can work with a relatively short sample, but quality improves with variety. Aim for at least five minutes of clean audio. Ten is better. The script should cover different sentence structures, emotional registers, and pacing. Read a few declarative statements. Read some questions. Read a short narrative passage. Read something with technical terminology.

Monotone delivery is a trap. If you read your entire script in the same flat tone, the model learns that flat tone as your voice. Speak naturally — speed up slightly where you'd naturally speed up, pause where you'd pause. Pretend you're talking to someone, not reading terms and conditions aloud.

## [The Three Mistakes That Produce Robotic Clones](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

**Recording in a live room.** Echo and reflection confuse the model. It captures the room as part of your voice.

**Inconsistent volume.** Leaning toward the mic for emphasis, then pulling back, creates level swings the model can't normalize cleanly. Keep your mouth-to-mic distance consistent — roughly a fist-width away.

**Mouth noise and breath artifacts.** Clicks, lip smacks, and loud breath intakes get learned too. Drink water before you record. Do a few jaw loosening exercises. Edit out any loud breaths during post-processing, or re-record those sentences.

None of this requires expensive equipment or a sound engineer. It requires about thirty minutes of setup and a second take on the sentences you stumbled through. Your clone will thank you.
