## [Start With Filters, Not the Search Bar](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Most people type a vague keyword into the search bar and get overwhelmed. Flip that approach. Before you type anything, lock in the structural filters first.

**Gender and age** knock out a huge portion of irrelevant results immediately. If you're casting a middle-aged male narrator, filter for male and middle-aged before you do anything else. The list shrinks fast.

**Accent** is the next filter that actually matters. "American" and "British" cover a lot of ground, but the library also surfaces Australian, Irish, South African, and other regional accents. Match the accent to your audience or your script's setting — don't just default to generic American because it's familiar.

**Use case tags** are underused. The library lets you filter by intended use — narration, conversational, news, characters, and so on. If you're building an audiobook, filter for narration first. You'll surface voices that are already tuned for long-form pacing.

## [Build a Shortlist Before You Commit](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Don't make decisions based on the default sample clip. Those clips are chosen to show range, not to match your content. Copy a sentence directly from your actual script and paste it as the audition text. A voice that sounds warm on the sample clip might sound flat or rushed on your specific copy.

Save promising voices to your shortlist as you go. ElevenLabs lets you bookmark voices in the library so you can compare them side by side later. Aim for three to five candidates before you pick one.

## [Matching Voice to Use Case](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Different projects pull in different directions.

**Audiobooks** need consistency over long durations. Look for voices tagged as narration with a slower default pace and clear consonants. Avoid anything described as "energetic" or "conversational" — those qualities fatigue listeners over chapters.

**Game characters** can go broader. Gravelly, accented, or stylized voices work well here because they reinforce character identity. Search by the character type you're building — villain, guide, merchant — and audition against actual in-game dialogue lines.

**Ads** need punch in the first three seconds. Filter for confident or authoritative voices, then test your headline copy, not your full script. If the voice doesn't land the first line, it won't rescue the rest.

One practical note: sort by "Most Used" when you're narrowing down. Voices that other creators have used extensively tend to hold up across different kinds of content and different text inputs. It's not a guarantee, but it's a useful signal.
