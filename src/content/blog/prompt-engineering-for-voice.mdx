Most people treat Voice Design like a search bar. They type "calm female voice" and wonder why the result sounds like a phone tree. The problem isn't the tool — it's the vocabulary. Prompting for voice is a skill, and like any skill, it gets sharper once you have the right words.

## [The Core Dimensions You're Actually Controlling](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Voice Design in [ElevenLabs](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) responds to a handful of distinct dimensions. Learn to address each one separately, and your prompts stop being vague wishes and start being specifications.

**Age** — Don't just say "young" or "old." Anchor it. "Late twenties" reads differently than "early forties." Age affects resonance, vocal weight, and how much wear a voice carries.

**Accent and dialect** — Be specific about region when it matters. "British accent" is nearly useless. "Northern English, working class" gives the model something to work with. Same goes for American regional speech — Southern, Midwestern, and coastal voices have meaningfully different textures.

**Pitch** — Use relative terms plus anchors. "Slightly lower than average for a woman, but not deep" lands better than just "low." If you have a reference archetype in mind (a late-night radio host, a documentary narrator), name it.

**Texture** — This is where most prompts fall apart. Texture describes the surface quality of the voice. Is it smooth? Breathy? Slightly gravelly? Crisp? These aren't decorative words — they change the output.

**Pacing** — Separate from the speed slider. Pacing in a prompt refers to the natural rhythm a speaker has: do they pause often? Do words run together? A "deliberate, unhurried speaker" sounds different from a "quick, clipped delivery" even at the same words-per-minute.

**Emotional register** — The baseline mood baked into the voice. Neutral is a register. So is warmly professional, dry and sardonic, or quietly authoritative. Name the register explicitly.

## [Reference Archetypes That Actually Work](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

Archetypes are shortcuts. Instead of building up every dimension from scratch, you describe a recognizable vocal type and then modify it.

Some that get reliable results:

- "True crime podcast host" — measured, slightly hushed, serious without being grim
- "Indie audiobook narrator" — intimate, unhurried, slight warmth
- "Corporate explainer video" — clear, neutral, confident without personality
- "Late-night jazz radio DJ" — smooth, low, relaxed cadence
- "Friendly customer service agent" — upbeat but not peppy, clean diction

Use these as a base, then layer in specifics. "True crime podcast host, female, mid-thirties, slight Southern accent, no breathiness" gives the model a clear target.

## [Putting It Together in Practice](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

A working prompt structure looks like this:

Age + gender + accent/region + pitch + texture + pacing + emotional register + archetype reference

Example: "Male, early fifties, neutral American accent, baritone, slightly gravelly, slow and deliberate, calm authority — documentary narrator."

That's eight pieces of information in one sentence. Each one is doing work.

One more thing: iterate. Generate a few variants from the same prompt, then adjust a single variable at a time. Treat it like mixing — change one fader before touching the next. You'll understand what each term is actually doing, and your next prompt will be better for it.

Voice Design rewards precision. The more clearly you can describe what you're hearing in your head, the closer [the output](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) will get to matching it.
