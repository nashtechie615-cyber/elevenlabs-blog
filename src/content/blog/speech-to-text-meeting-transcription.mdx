## [Skip the Bot, Keep the Record](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

ElevenLabs Scribe is a speech-to-text model you can use without sending a bot into your meetings. The workflow is simple: record the audio locally on your own device, then upload the file directly to [Scribe](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4) for transcription. No third-party app joining your Zoom. No permissions to negotiate with your IT department. No notification popping up that says "Otter.ai has joined the call."

This matters more than it sounds. Some clients have strict policies about recording tools on their calls. Some conversations are sensitive enough that you'd rather not route audio through a service that integrates directly with your video platform. And honestly, some people just find it rude. Recording locally and uploading afterward keeps the meeting feeling like a meeting.

Most operating systems and video platforms have native local recording built in. Zoom, Google Meet, and Teams all let the host record to disk. QuickTime works on Mac. Audacity works everywhere. You get a clean audio file, and Scribe handles the rest.

## [Speaker Diarization That Actually Works](https://try.elevenlabs.io/8jq9xhb1mno4)

The part that usually falls apart with transcription tools is speaker identification. You get a wall of text with no sense of who said what, and then you spend twenty minutes manually sorting it out. Scribe includes speaker diarization — it identifies and labels different speakers in the audio automatically.

It's not perfect in every scenario. Overlapping voices, poor microphone quality, and large groups all create challenges for any diarization system. But in a standard two-to-six person meeting recorded with decent audio, Scribe separates speakers cleanly enough to be genuinely useful without heavy editing afterward.

The output gives you a transcript organized by speaker turns, which makes it straightforward to pull out action items, summarize decisions, or hand the file to another tool for further processing.
